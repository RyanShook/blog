I have spent the better part of two years pushing my team to use AI more. Better summaries from staff meetings. Faster grant drafts. Smarter inbox triage. Most weeks I am the one telling a director, "yes, that is fine to run through Claude, ship it." So it surprises some people when I am also the one in the room saying "no, not for this."

The line is not where I expected it to be a year ago. It is not really about accuracy or hallucinations or even data privacy, though all three matter. It is about trust, and specifically the kind of trust that ministry runs on. There is a category of work in church and nonprofit life where introducing AI does measurable damage to the relationship even when nobody on the other side ever finds out.

Here are the three places I have decided AI does not get to touch in our shop, and the test I use when something new shows up.

## Pastoral notes and member care

Every CHMS I have used (Planning Center, Rock, Breeze) lets you attach private notes to a person's profile. Pastoral conversations end up there. So do prayer requests, hard family situations, bereavement details, and the kinds of things people say when they are trying to figure out whether the church is a safe place to be honest.

The temptation is real. You take a 45-minute coffee meeting with a member, come back to your desk, and a tool that can turn rough notes into a tidy 200-word summary is sitting right there. I have watched staff do this once or twice and I get it. The summaries are good.

I still tell our team no. Three reasons.

First, those notes are the most sensitive data the church holds. Most of the AI tools we have access to are fine on data handling for regular work, but I do not want member care notes leaving our systems for a third-party model unless we have an enterprise data agreement specifically covering that use, which we mostly do not.

Second, the act of writing the note is part of the pastoring. When a pastor sits with their own thoughts after a hard conversation and tries to put them into words, they remember things. They notice patterns. The Holy Spirit prompts something. A tool that hands you a clean summary skips that step, and you end up with cleaner records and shallower care.

Third, if a member ever found out that the substance of their hardest conversation got fed to a chatbot, even one that handled it perfectly, they would feel it as a small betrayal. They would not be wrong.

## Sermon thinking you plan to call your own

I am not opposed to AI in sermon prep. Background research, cross-references, a check on whether I have the historical context right, a quick "what are the common counterarguments to this point" pass before I commit to it. Those are all fair game and useful. I use them.

What I will not do, and what I push back on with our preaching team, is letting a model write the actual line of thought. The pastoral promise behind preaching is that the person standing up there has wrestled with this passage, brought their own life and study to it, and is offering the congregation what God has given them through that wrestle. If the structure and the turns and the application points came from a prompt, that promise is broken regardless of how good the output is.

The honest test I use: could I tell the congregation, plainly, where this thought came from? "I asked Claude to outline three possible structures and picked one" is not a sentence I want to say from a stage. So I do not.

## Donor moments that need a human voice

There is a difference between donor operations and donor relationships. Operations work (gift acknowledgment letters, year-end tax statements, reactivation campaigns where everyone is getting roughly the same message) is fine to draft with AI and edit by hand. Most of our acknowledgment workflow goes through a Claude pass before it goes out. The text is better for it.

But there are moments in donor work that are not operations. The phone call after a major gift. The handwritten note when someone gives sacrificially out of a hard year. The personal text after a big estate-planning conversation. Those are relationship moments, and they belong to the person whose name is on them.

The test is simple. If a donor's first reaction to reading the message would be "did Ryan actually write this," I should not be using a model to write it. The same logic applies to volunteer thank-yous, leadership development emails, and anything pastoral going to a single named person.

## The calm test

I keep a one-line gate for new use cases. When someone on the team brings me a "could we use AI for X" idea, I ask: "if the person on the other end found out we used AI here, would we be calm about telling them?"

If the answer is "yes, we would explain it cleanly and they would shrug," it is probably a fine use. Drafting the volunteer-onboarding email everyone gets, summarizing the elder meeting, building a search interface for our policy library, drafting a grant report first pass, cleaning up the donor database, generating slide outlines from a sermon transcript. All of those pass. None of them are pretending to be something they are not.

If the answer is "we would never want them to know," the use is not fine, and the discomfort is data. It usually means the AI is being used to fake a relationship or a thought process that the recipient was promised was human.

## Where this leaves us

The list of things I tell my team to use AI for is much, much longer than the list of things I tell them not to. Operations, research, drafting, triage, analysis, design, software, automation. Almost the whole stack. We probably save 12 to 15 hours a week across our team because of it.

But the three carve-outs above (pastoral notes, sermon thinking, personal donor moments) are firm, and I do not see them moving. The reason is not technical. It is that ministry is, at root, a set of promises about presence. AI is not present. It is good at almost everything else, and it is worth all the time and money we put into it. It just is not, and cannot be, the person in the room.

That is the line. Hold it cheerfully, and you can use AI everywhere else without flinching.
